Research Domain

This section presents the research background, identified gap, core problem, objectives, and methodology behind the AI-based system developed for Sri Lankan medicinal plants.

Literature Survey

Artificial intelligence has shown strong potential in medicinal-plant recognition, especially through deep learning and transfer learning models that classify plants from images with high accuracy. The reviewed studies, including both international and Sri Lankan work, show that CNN-based approaches are effective when suitable training data is available.

However, most earlier systems were built around global datasets or leaf-only recognition. That makes them less reliable for Sri Lankan medicinal plants and less useful in real medicinal settings where the correct plant part, plant condition, and practical field use also matter. As a result, the literature points toward the need for a more localized and complete decision-support approach.

Source: Literature Review, pp. 1–2.

Research Gap

The main gap in existing research is not only about model accuracy, but about completeness. Many systems can identify a plant species, yet very few combine localized Sri Lankan data, plant-part segmentation, quality evaluation, post-harvest guidance, and usability for non-technical users in one platform.

This means farmers, herbal practitioners, and students may still need to rely on separate tools or manual judgment even after a species is recognized. The paper therefore positions this project as a response to the missing integration between recognition, quality assessment, and practical decision support.

Source: Literature Review, pp. 1–2; Introduction, p. 1.

Research Problem

Sri Lanka’s medicinal plant sector depends on correct plant identification, proper harvesting, good nutrient management, and careful post-harvest handling. When any of these steps go wrong, medicinal potency and economic value can drop, and safe practical use becomes more difficult.

The core problem is therefore broader than species recognition alone. A useful system must also check plant quality, understand which plant part is relevant, and provide actionable guidance on harvesting, fertilizer use, drying, and storage. This project addresses that wider real-world problem through one integrated AI workflow.

Source: Abstract and Introduction, p. 1.

Research Objectives
  • To develop a localized AI workflow that identifies Sri Lankan medicinal plant species and evaluates visible plant health.
  • To detect and segment the correct medicinal plant parts so the system supports safer and more accurate usage.
  • To provide practical decision support for harvest timing, fertilizer selection, drying, and storage.
  • To deploy the complete solution as a lightweight web platform that remains useful in both rural and urban contexts.

In full, the project also targets cause detection for poor plant condition, such as disease, pests, nutrient stress, or environmental impact, and connects those findings with recommendation logic for real-world use.

Source: Abstract and Introduction, p. 1; Methodology, pp. 2–3.

Methodology

The system follows a structured AI pipeline starting from data collection, preprocessing, model training, and deployment. A localized dataset of Sri Lankan medicinal plants was collected and used to train deep learning models for plant identification, quality assessment, and segmentation.

The system consists of four main modules: plant identification and quality assessment, plant part segmentation, post-harvest advisory, and fertilizer recommendation with harvest prediction. These modules are integrated into a lightweight web-based platform for real-time usage.
