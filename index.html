Our Research

Research Domain

The research background, identified gaps, problem statement, objectives, and methodology behind the AI-based system developed for Sri Lankan medicinal plants.

01 Literature Survey

Artificial Intelligence and Deep Learning have transformed plant analysis by enabling automated species recognition, disease diagnosis, quality evaluation, and predictive decision support. Prior studies have successfully applied CNN architectures such as ResNet, EfficientNet, MobileNet, and transfer learning models for medicinal plant classification with high accuracy.

Research in semantic and instance segmentation using U-Net, DeepLabV3+, and Mask R-CNN has further improved the precise extraction of plant structures, medicinal parts, and processed products. These approaches support fine-grained classification and quality analysis.

Beyond image recognition, smart agriculture research shows the value of machine learning, remote sensing, IoT sensors, and predictive analytics for fertilizer optimization, harvest prediction, drying guidance, and storage monitoring.

However, most existing systems solve isolated problems and lack an integrated, Sri Lanka-specific platform that combines plant identification, segmentation, quality assessment, and advisory support in one intelligent framework.

02 Research Gap

Although previous studies report strong model performance, major gaps remain in localization, practical usability, and system integration. Most available solutions depend on global datasets that do not adequately represent Sri Lankan medicinal flora.

Existing systems often focus only on species recognition while ignoring critical tasks such as medicinal plant-part segmentation, visual quality grading, nutrient recommendation, harvest timing prediction, and post-harvest preservation support.

There is also a lack of end-to-end intelligent systems capable of linking these functions together. For example, the identified plant type should support fertilizer advice, harvest recommendations, drying duration, and storage guidance.

Another important gap is accessibility. Many models remain as research prototypes and are not optimized for lightweight deployment suitable for farmers, herbal practitioners, students, and rural users.

03 Research Problem

Sri Lanka’s medicinal plant sector faces challenges in accurate identification, quality assurance, cultivation support, and preservation of medicinal potency. Traditional practices often rely on expert judgement, making the process inconsistent, time-consuming, and difficult to scale.

Misidentification of plants or plant parts can reduce treatment reliability, while improper fertilizer use, poor harvest timing, and weak drying or storage practices can reduce medicinal quality and increase economic losses.

The problem therefore extends beyond recognition accuracy alone. A practical solution must support the full lifecycle of medicinal plants from cultivation guidance to quality validation and post-harvest decision-making.

This research addresses how an integrated AI-driven platform can solve these interconnected problems through computer vision, machine learning, IoT-supported monitoring, and advisory intelligence.

04 Research Objectives
  • Develop a localized intelligent system for identifying Sri Lankan medicinal plants and products.
  • Implement segmentation models to detect relevant medicinal plant parts accurately.
  • Develop quality assessment mechanisms to analyze visible plant or product conditions.
  • Provide intelligent fertilizer recommendation and harvest timing support.
  • Generate post-harvest drying and storage recommendations to reduce spoilage and preserve quality.
  • Integrate all modules within a unified web-based decision support platform.

The broader objective is to improve productivity, preserve medicinal quality, support Ayurveda practices, and provide accessible technology support to farmers, students, herbal practitioners, and researchers.

05 Methodology

The proposed methodology follows a multi-stage research pipeline including data collection, preprocessing, annotation, model development, system integration, evaluation, and deployment.

A localized medicinal plant dataset is collected from field visits, herbal markets, plant nurseries, and curated sources. Images are prepared for plant identification, plant-part segmentation, and quality assessment tasks.

Deep learning models are trained for recognition, segmentation, and quality grading, while machine learning models support fertilizer advisory, harvest prediction, drying duration estimation, and storage recommendation generation.

The system consists of four intelligent modules working together: Plant Identification and Quality Assessment, Plant Part Segmentation, Fertilizer and Harvest Advisory, and Post-Harvest Drying and Storage Advisory.

Outputs from these modules are integrated through a lightweight web platform designed for real-time user interaction and practical deployment.
